This document defines how this trust relates to place, location, observation, local context, missions, public visibility, and the wider AI Freedom Trust Federation map.

The Living Atlas is not merely a map of places. It is a local-first stewardship interface that helps a trust understand where it is, what it is responsible for, what is nearby, what is active, what can be shared, what must remain private, and how local work connects to the larger Federation.

## Core Principle

The Living Atlas centers the trust in its own frame of reference without making it the center of everyone else's reality.

Each trust experiences its own Green Zone as the local present: the place where identity, permission, observation, relationship, current work, local needs, and live context meet. Other trusts retain their own centers, boundaries, privacy, and sovereignty.

The Atlas should reveal relationship without erasing consent.

## Green Zone

The Green Zone is the trust's local present layer.

It may include:

- current steward context;
- approximate place;
- active branches of the Tree of Life;
- nearby missions;
- trusted contacts;
- local projects;
- community needs;
- available resources;
- time-sensitive tasks;
- safe public points of contact;
- private records needed for local stewardship;
- Federation nodes the trust chooses to see or share with.

The Green Zone is not automatically public. It begins private and becomes shared only by consent.

## Location Sovereignty

Location belongs to the trust's privacy boundary.

The trust may choose different location precision levels for different purposes. No map, AI steward, marketplace, mission board, public profile, or Federation service should assume permission to expose precise location.

Location precision levels include:

- **private only** — location remains local and unpublished;
- **approximate region** — broad regional visibility without precise placement;
- **city-level** — public or shared city context;
- **neighborhood-level** — approximate neighborhood context for local missions or community discovery;
- **public project location** — a location intentionally shared for a project, event, service, or public-good mission;
- **public business location** — a lawful public business or service address;
- **temporary event location** — time-limited visibility for a mission, event, meeting, delivery, repair, or community action;
- **repository-only presence** — public work is visible through repositories without geographic disclosure;
- **Federation-only visibility** — visible to selected Federation members under defined permissions.

The trust may use one precision level for public discovery, another for trusted collaborators, and another for private AI-assisted planning.

## Atlas Layers

The Living Atlas may organize reality through layers. Layers should be permissioned and inspectable.

### Identity Layer

Shows the trust's public or private identity according to visibility settings. This may include trust name, display name, mission, maturity, Federation status, and public contact method.

### Relationship Layer

Represents trusted circles, contributors, advisors, collaborators, customers, served communities, partner trusts, and Federation connections. Relationship visibility must respect consent.

### Tree of Life Layer

Maps seeds, roots, trunk, branches, leaves, fruit, and new seeds. This layer connects location and work to the trust's growth model.

### Mission Layer

Shows missions, tasks, bounties, service needs, repairs, events, local actions, public goods, educational work, and active opportunities.

### Repository Layer

Connects software, documents, schemas, prompts, issues, pull requests, releases, and public code to the branches they support.

### Economy Layer

Shows lawful services, contribution flows, grants, bounties, marketplace listings, cooperative support, payments, and completed value where the trust chooses to publish them.

### Resource Layer

Represents tools, spaces, equipment, inventories, vehicles, gardens, kitchens, freezers, workshops, infrastructure, knowledge bases, or other resources the trust stewards or may share.

### Event Layer

Represents meetings, gatherings, service days, deliveries, ceremonies, classes, launches, review cycles, and time-bound missions.

### Risk and Safety Layer

Privately helps the trust understand safety concerns, sensitive locations, restricted records, emergency contacts, data exposure risks, and boundaries. This layer should be private by default.

### Federation Layer

Shows other trusts, public Federation nodes, public projects, open missions, shared standards, and voluntary points of cooperation.

## Public Discovery

The trust may choose to publish selected information for discovery.

Public discovery should not expose private life unnecessarily. A useful public node may include:

- trust display name;
- public mission statement;
- public trust type;
- approximate location precision;
- public projects;
- public repositories;
- public services or offerings;
- public missions;
- contribution history;
- contact method;
- Federation Link status.

Public discovery should clearly distinguish verified claims from self-declared claims and experimental work from mature production systems.

## Privacy and Consent

The Atlas must protect privacy by design.

The following must not be made public without explicit consent and lawful purpose:

- precise private location;
- home address;
- private family locations;
- safety-sensitive routes;
- medical, legal, custody, or financial records;
- private correspondence;
- private Green Zone context;
- minors' location or identity information;
- private meeting locations;
- credentials, access tokens, keys, or security data;
- sensitive community vulnerabilities.

Consent should identify what is shared, with whom, for what purpose, for how long, and at what precision.

## Atlas Records

The trust should preserve records for important Atlas actions.

Useful records include:

- location visibility settings;
- published public nodes;
- mission records;
- completed local actions;
- Atlas-related consent decisions;
- Federation Link changes;
- public profile changes;
- event records;
- resource records;
- safety reviews;
- removed or archived nodes.

Records should make it clear what is private, shared, public, expired, archived, or under review.

## AI Steward Role

The AI steward may assist the Living Atlas by:

- summarizing local context;
- organizing missions;
- classifying location sensitivity;
- drafting public profiles;
- identifying nearby opportunities when given permission;
- connecting repositories to branches and missions;
- reviewing privacy risks;
- preparing mission checklists;
- summarizing completed fruit;
- helping maintain Green Zone records.

The AI steward must not publish location, infer private location for public use, expose sensitive places, create public Atlas nodes, or change visibility settings without explicit human approval.

## Federation Nodes

A Federation node is a trust, project, repository, mission, resource, service, event, or public-good effort that chooses to participate in the wider Atlas.

Nodes should have:

- name;
- type;
- purpose;
- steward or contact method;
- visibility level;
- maturity level;
- relationship to a trust;
- relationship to the Tree of Life;
- location precision;
- privacy classification;
- evidence or provenance where relevant.

A node may be private, shared, public, temporary, archived, experimental, verified, unverified, or disputed.

## Maturity and Reliability Labels

Atlas entries should avoid overstating readiness.

Suggested maturity labels include:

- idea;
- draft;
- experimental;
- local pilot;
- active;
- reviewed;
- public;
- mature;
- archived;
- deprecated;
- disputed;
- emergency.

Reliability labels should help users distinguish verified information, self-reported information, AI-generated summaries, public records, and unreviewed drafts.

## Relationship to Economy

The Living Atlas may support lawful economic activity by helping people discover services, products, missions, bounties, grants, resources, cooperative needs, and completed fruit.

Economic visibility must remain truthful and lawful. The Atlas should not present speculative value, token claims, investment opportunities, financial performance, licensing status, or regulated offerings without appropriate verification and qualified review.

## Relationship to Governance

Changes to public Atlas visibility are governance actions.

Material changes should be recorded, especially when they affect privacy, public identity, location, reputation, economic participation, Federation status, or another person's rights.

The trust may revoke or reduce visibility when needed to protect safety, privacy, mission integrity, or lawful responsibility.

## Practical Review Questions

Before publishing or updating an Atlas entry, the trust should ask:

1. What exactly are we sharing?
2. Who can see it?
3. What location precision is necessary?
4. Does this expose anyone else's private information?
5. Is the claim verified, self-declared, experimental, or uncertain?
6. Does the entry connect to a branch, mission, fruit, or new seed?
7. What harm could occur if this is misunderstood or misused?
8. Should this visibility expire?
9. What record should be preserved?
10. Does this strengthen trustworthiness and useful cooperation?

## Amendment

This Living Atlas constitution may be amended through the trust's governance process.

Amendments should improve clarity, privacy, safety, interoperability, local sovereignty, mission discovery, Green Zone stewardship, and the relationship between place and useful work.

Each amendment should be documented and versioned.

## Closing Declaration

This trust uses the Living Atlas to see its place without surrendering its privacy, to cooperate without losing sovereignty, to discover needs without exploiting people, and to connect local work to the larger Federation through consent, evidence, stewardship, and useful contribution.
