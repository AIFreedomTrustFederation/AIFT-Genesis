This document defines how this trust makes decisions, grants permission, accepts responsibility, handles conflict, preserves records, and changes its own rules.

Governance is the practice of stewardship under accountability. It is not merely bureaucracy, control, voting, hierarchy, automation, or legal paperwork. Governance is how the trust turns values into decisions that can be understood, reviewed, corrected, and inherited.

## Governance Purpose

The purpose of governance is to protect human dignity, preserve trust sovereignty, clarify authority, prevent hidden control, support useful work, resolve conflict, document material decisions, and keep the trust aligned with its mission.

Good governance should help the trust answer:

- who may decide;
- what they may decide;
- what evidence is required;
- whose consent is required;
- what records must be preserved;
- what risks must be reviewed;
- how decisions may be challenged, corrected, or amended.

## Foundational Governance Principles

This trust inherits the following governance principles:

1. Authority exists for stewardship, not domination.
2. Human dignity and agency remain prior to software, ledgers, maps, markets, and AI systems.
3. Privacy, consent, and safety require explicit protection.
4. Decisions should be proportionate to risk.
5. Higher-risk decisions require stronger review, clearer records, and more explicit consent.
6. AI may assist governance but may not become the governing authority.
7. Governance should be local-first, inspectable, versioned, and recoverable.
8. Conflicts should be handled through truth, evidence, accountability, repair, and lawful process where necessary.
9. Economic, legal, medical, safety, custody, and identity-sensitive decisions require heightened care.
10. Amendments should improve stewardship without erasing accountability or institutional memory.

## Steward Roles

A trust may include multiple roles. A role is a stewardship responsibility, not an identity of superiority.

### Primary Steward

The primary steward is responsible for maintaining the trust's continuity, mission alignment, records, local sovereignty, and final approval of high-risk actions unless governance is later expanded.

A generated trust begins with the assumption that the primary steward has authority over ordinary local operations while remaining accountable to this governance constitution.

### Co-Stewards

Co-stewards may share responsibility for projects, records, assets, relationships, operations, repositories, ministries, businesses, families, or community work.

Co-steward authority should be documented by scope. A co-steward may have authority over one branch of the Tree of Life without having authority over the entire trust.

### Advisors

Advisors may provide counsel, review, technical input, legal input, financial input, spiritual counsel, operational guidance, safety planning, or conflict mediation.

Advisors do not receive decision authority unless the trust explicitly grants it.

### Contributors

Contributors provide useful work. Contributions may include code, documentation, research, design, service, labor, care, funding, review, teaching, mentoring, repair, public goods, or mission execution.

Contribution does not automatically create governance authority, ownership, employment, partnership, legal representation, or economic entitlement unless separately documented.

### Beneficiaries or Served Community

Beneficiaries and served communities are the people, families, customers, users, neighbors, contributors, clients, future generations, or public communities the trust exists to serve.

The trust should consider the effect of decisions on beneficiaries, especially when they cannot advocate for themselves.

### AI Steward

The AI steward may assist with governance records, drafting, summarization, issue tracking, risk review, decision logs, and consistency checks.

The AI steward does not possess independent constitutional authority. It may not grant itself permissions, change governance, publish private information, create financial commitments, or make legal claims without human approval.

## Decision Classes

Governance should classify decisions before acting.

### Routine Decisions

Routine decisions are low-risk operational actions that do not alter governance, expose sensitive information, create material commitments, or affect legal, financial, medical, identity, custody, safety, or public-reputation matters.

Examples include minor document edits, task organization, ordinary issue comments, local notes, draft planning, formatting cleanup, non-sensitive research organization, and routine repository maintenance.

Default rule: routine decisions may be made by the steward or delegated contributor responsible for the relevant scope. AI may assist with routine decisions when operating under approved permissions.

### Material Decisions

Material decisions affect the trust's public identity, mission, money, assets, governance, relationships, sensitive records, reputation, repository structure, formal commitments, or Federation participation.

Examples include publishing public profiles, entering partnerships, accepting significant funds, creating public claims, changing permission boundaries, releasing software, changing economic rules, granting repository access, or committing the trust to ongoing obligations.

Default rule: material decisions require explicit human approval from the responsible steward and should leave an auditable record.

### High-Risk Decisions

High-risk decisions involve legal, financial, medical, safety, custody, family, regulated economic, private identity, security, or public reputation consequences.

Examples include legal representations, investment or securities-related claims, health-related claims, private family records, custody-sensitive information, safety plans, precise private location, credentials, private keys, regulated services, and public allegations of misconduct.

Default rule: high-risk decisions require explicit human approval, documented reasoning, evidence review, and qualified professional guidance when appropriate.

### Emergency Decisions

Emergency decisions address immediate threats to safety, account integrity, system integrity, privacy, funds, keys, reputation, or critical infrastructure.

Examples include account compromise, credential exposure, active harassment, urgent safety risk, infrastructure failure, accidental publication of private data, or urgent repository security issue.

Default rule: the responsible steward may take the least invasive action necessary to reduce immediate harm, then document the action, notify affected people where appropriate, and review the decision after the emergency.

## Consent Rules

The trust should use consent that is specific, informed, voluntary, documented where needed, and proportionate to risk.

The following require explicit human approval:

- publishing private or sensitive information;
- changing governance documents;
- changing AI permissions, memory, tools, or write access;
- creating public commitments or representations;
- spending shared resources beyond routine thresholds;
- changing legal, financial, medical, identity, custody, family, safety, or reputation-sensitive claims;
- sharing precise location or private Green Zone information;
- granting access to repositories, credentials, accounts, keys, or private archives;
- initiating regulated economic activity;
- linking the trust publicly to the Federation.

Consent may be limited by purpose, time, audience, risk level, or scope. A permission granted for one purpose must not be stretched into unrelated authority.

## Delegation

The trust may delegate authority for specific tasks.

Every delegation should define:

- the person, role, AI agent, repository, or system receiving authority;
- the scope of authority;
- the actions allowed;
- the actions prohibited;
- the time period or review cycle;
- the records required;
- the process for revocation.

Delegation should be narrow enough to protect the trust and clear enough to allow useful work.

## AI Governance Boundaries

AI may support governance but must remain subordinate to documented human authority.

AI may help:

- summarize discussions;
- draft decisions;
- identify risks;
- compare documents for consistency;
- maintain issue lists;
- generate proposed amendments;
- organize records;
- explain tradeoffs;
- prepare review packets.

AI must not independently:

- approve material or high-risk decisions;
- publish private data;
- create legal, financial, medical, safety, custody, or regulated claims;
- grant itself new permissions;
- alter governance documents without human approval;
- impersonate a human steward;
- conceal uncertainty, missing evidence, or tool limitations;
- represent speculation as verified record.

## Records and Decision Logs

Material, high-risk, emergency, and governance-changing decisions should be recorded.

A good decision record includes:

- date;
- decision title;
- decision class;
- responsible steward or approving party;
- affected documents, repositories, systems, or people;
- summary of the decision;
- evidence reviewed;
- risks considered;
- consent obtained;
- implementation steps;
- follow-up review date if needed.

Git commits, issues, pull requests, signed notes, meeting summaries, decision logs, receipts, and manifests may all serve as governance records.

## Contribution Governance

The trust welcomes useful contribution when aligned with its mission and values.

Contributions should be honest, documented, reviewable, and connected to real value. Contributors should not be promised ownership, payment, employment, partnership, governance power, token value, legal status, or future benefit unless those terms are explicitly documented.

Repository contributions should be reviewed according to project maturity, risk, and public impact.

## Economic Governance

Economic activity must serve the trust's mission and remain bounded by truth, law, consent, and clear records.

The trust should distinguish between donations, purchases, service payments, grants, rewards, memberships, wages, contractor payments, loans, investments, securities, insurance, banking, and speculative assets.

Regulated activity requires qualified review before public deployment or promotion.

Financial commitments should identify:

- payer and recipient;
- purpose;
- amount or value;
- timing;
- obligations;
- refund, cancellation, or dispute terms where relevant;
- applicable legal or tax considerations requiring outside review.

## Federation Governance

Participation in the AI Freedom Trust Federation is voluntary.

The trust may choose what to publish, synchronize, verify, share, or withhold. Federation services may provide standards, tools, maps, repositories, missions, marketplaces, learning, and mutual aid, but they do not erase local sovereignty.

The trust may change its Federation visibility through its own governance process.

## Conflict and Repair

Conflict should be handled with truth, restraint, boundaries, accountability, and repair.

The default path for ordinary conflict is:

1. clarify the issue;
2. identify affected people and records;
3. distinguish facts, interpretations, emotions, and requested remedies;
4. review evidence;
5. acknowledge harm or uncertainty;
6. seek repair where possible;
7. document the decision or resolution;
8. escalate to qualified help when needed.

When safety, abuse, coercion, fraud, exploitation, harassment, threats, custody risk, legal risk, or serious misconduct are involved, the trust should prioritize protection, documentation, appropriate boundaries, and lawful process.

Repair does not require denial of harm. Forgiveness does not erase accountability. Boundaries do not erase dignity.

## Removal, Revocation, and Suspension

The trust may revoke permissions, suspend access, remove a contributor from a scope of work, archive a branch, disconnect a tool, rotate credentials, or withdraw from a Federation service when necessary to protect privacy, safety, integrity, mission, or lawful responsibility.

Such actions should be documented when safe and appropriate.

## Amendment Process

This governance constitution may be amended when change is necessary to improve clarity, accountability, stewardship, privacy, consent, safety, interoperability, or mission alignment.

A governance amendment should include:

- proposed change;
- reason for change;
- affected sections;
- risk review;
- approving steward or governance body;
- effective date;
- version or commit reference.

Default rule: amendments require explicit approval by the primary steward or the documented governance body responsible for the trust.

The amendment history should be preserved through Git or another auditable record system.

## Constitutional Priority

If governance procedures conflict with the trust's core constitutional commitments, human dignity, privacy, consent, truthfulness, and lawful responsibility should guide interpretation.

If there is uncertainty, the trust should slow down, preserve records, seek review, and choose the path that best protects agency, safety, transparency, and long-term trustworthiness.

## Closing Declaration

This trust governs itself so that stewardship can be practiced with clarity, courage, accountability, humility, and care. Governance should not become empty control. It should become the living discipline by which the trust protects what matters, repairs what breaks, and grows in service to people, communities, and future generations.
