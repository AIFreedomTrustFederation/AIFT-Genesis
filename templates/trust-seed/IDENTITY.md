# Identity Constitution

This document defines how identity is created, protected, represented, shared, and amended within this trust.

Identity in the AI Freedom Trust Federation is not merely a username, profile, credential, wallet, email address, public page, or legal name. Identity is the relationship between a steward, a trust, its records, its values, its permissions, its history, its public presence, and its voluntary connection to the wider Federation.

A trust should be able to exist locally before it exists publicly. Public identity is an intentional disclosure, not the source of the trust's existence.

## Identity Principles

This trust inherits the following identity principles:

1. Identity begins locally and privately.
2. Public visibility is optional and permissioned.
3. Legal identity, trust identity, public identity, and AI-agent identity must remain distinguishable.
4. A person is never reduced to a profile, score, wallet, credential, or data object.
5. Credentials should support stewardship without becoming tools of coercion or exclusion.
6. Reputation should arise from transparent contribution, not manipulation, status games, or empty speculation.
7. Private identity data must not be published merely because it is technically available.
8. AI agents may assist with identity records but may not define, expose, or alter identity without permission.
9. Identity records should be portable, inspectable, recoverable, and amendable where practical.
10. Every identity claim should be labeled according to evidence, source, verification status, and visibility.

## Identity Layers

This trust recognizes four primary identity layers.

### 1. Local Identity

Local identity is the private identity layer controlled by the trust.

It may include private names, legal names, addresses, contact information, private relationships, identity documents, credentials, keys, records, recovery information, and sensitive context needed for lawful or practical stewardship.

Local identity remains private by default. It should be stored in local or permissioned systems and should not be synchronized, indexed, published, or exposed to AI tools unless explicitly permitted.

### 2. Trust Identity

Trust identity is the operational identity of the trust itself.

It includes the trust name, mission, values, stewardship scope, governance structure, Tree of Life, public maturity labels, repository links, contribution records, and declared relationship to the Federation.

Trust identity may be public, private, or shared depending on the trust's own configuration.

### 3. Public Federation Identity

Public Federation identity is the optional profile or node the trust chooses to reveal to the wider Federation.

It may include a public display name, public mission statement, public projects, repositories, offerings, missions, contributions, location precision, and Federation Link settings.

Public Federation identity should reveal only what the trust intentionally chooses to publish.

### 4. AI Steward Identity

AI steward identity defines the role, name, permissions, boundaries, memory scope, and accountability rules for any AI assistant operating inside the trust.

An AI steward is not the trust, not the human steward, not a legal authority, and not an owner. It is a tool of stewardship operating under documented permission.

## Visibility Classifications

Every identity record should be classified before use.

### Private Local

Stored locally and not shared by default. Examples include legal documents, private notes, precise addresses, private family information, internal financial records, recovery keys, and sensitive correspondence.

### AI-Readable Local

Available to the trust's AI steward for limited assistance. These records remain private and may not be published or transmitted outside the approved tool context without additional consent.

### Shared Circle

Shared with selected people, stewards, advisors, collaborators, or trusted groups for a defined purpose.

### Public Federation

Published intentionally to the wider Federation for discovery, collaboration, reputation, missions, marketplace participation, repository work, or public accountability.

### Regulated or Sensitive

Requires special caution and may require qualified review before use. This includes legal identity data, medical data, financial data, custody or family-sensitive data, safety-sensitive data, government identity records, regulated credentials, investment-related information, and precise private location.

## Green Zone Identity

The Green Zone is the trust's local present layer: the place where identity, location, current responsibilities, relationships, permissions, and live context meet.

Green Zone identity may include approximate geography, active projects, local missions, community relationships, trusted contacts, and current work. It should never require public exposure of precise private location.

The trust may choose a location precision level, such as private only, approximate region, city-level, neighborhood-level, public project location, public business location, or temporary event-specific visibility.

## Credentials and Verification

Credentials may help a trust prove claims, qualifications, roles, relationships, or permissions.

Examples include professional credentials, organizational roles, licenses, repository permissions, contribution records, signed attestations, educational records, business registrations, and other verifiable records.

A credential should be labeled according to its source and verification status:

- self-declared;
- trust-attested;
- peer-attested;
- document-supported;
- repository-supported;
- organization-verified;
- legally verified;
- expired, revoked, or disputed.

The trust must not present unverified claims as verified credentials.

## Reputation and Provenance

Reputation should be built from transparent contribution and trustworthy conduct.

Relevant reputation signals may include:

- completed missions;
- merged code;
- reviewed documentation;
- published research;
- maintained repositories;
- verified services delivered;
- mentorship;
- governance participation;
- repair work;
- public goods;
- long-term stewardship.

Reputation is not a substitute for consent, law, professional licensing, due process, or safety review.

Provenance should be preserved wherever practical. Important claims should point to their source, such as a Git commit, document, issue, receipt, signed attestation, public record, or stewarded archive.

## Identity Security

The trust must protect identity-sensitive information.

The following should never be casually published:

- legal names where not necessary;
- home addresses or precise private location;
- government identity numbers;
- private keys, recovery phrases, passwords, tokens, or access credentials;
- medical information;
- financial account information;
- custody, family, or safety-sensitive records;
- private correspondence;
- biometric data;
- private information about minors or vulnerable people.

When disclosure is necessary, it should be limited, documented, purpose-bound, and reviewed.

## Identity Lifecycle

Identity evolves over time.

### Creation

A trust begins with a local identity record and a trust identity. Public Federation identity is optional.

### Amendment

Identity records may be corrected, expanded, or reclassified through the trust's governance process.

### Recovery

The trust should maintain a recovery path for important identity records, keys, documents, and permissions.

### Archival

Historical identity records should be preserved when they matter for accountability, provenance, or continuity, while respecting privacy and safety.

### Retirement

A trust may retire, archive, transfer, fork, or close parts of its identity according to governance, law, and stewardship responsibilities.

## AI Steward Identity Rules

An AI steward may help organize, summarize, classify, draft, and search identity records only within approved scopes.

The AI steward must ask for human approval before:

- publishing identity information;
- changing legal, financial, medical, custody, safety, or reputation-sensitive identity records;
- expanding its own memory or permissions;
- sharing private identity data with external services;
- creating public claims about credentials, roles, status, or authority;
- changing location visibility.

The AI steward must preserve uncertainty and must not invent credentials, relationships, approvals, legal status, or verification.

## Public Profile Standard

A public Federation identity should be understandable without exposing private life.

A healthy public profile may include:

- display name;
- mission statement;
- trust type;
- public projects;
- public repositories;
- public missions;
- public contribution history;
- public contact method;
- public location precision chosen by the trust;
- public maturity labels;
- Federation Link status.

A public profile should not imply legal, financial, medical, professional, or investment status beyond what is actually verified.

## Amendment

This identity constitution may be amended through the trust's governance process.

Amendments should improve clarity, privacy, consent, verification, portability, recovery, and interoperability without weakening human dignity or local sovereignty.

Each amendment should be documented and versioned so identity history remains inspectable.

## Closing Declaration

This trust recognizes identity as sacred stewardship rather than mere data. Identity must be protected, clarified, verified where appropriate, shared only by consent, and used to strengthen human agency, trustworthiness, accountability, and voluntary cooperation within the AI Freedom Trust Federation.
