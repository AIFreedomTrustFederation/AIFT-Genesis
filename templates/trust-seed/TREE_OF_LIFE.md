This document defines the growth model of this trust.

The Tree of Life is the trust's living pattern of origin, nourishment, mission, work, contribution, completed value, and future replication. It gives human stewards, AI stewards, repositories, missions, projects, and communities a shared way to understand how the trust grows without becoming extractive, chaotic, rootless, or merely performative.

The Tree of Life is not a metaphor only. It is an operational map for stewardship.

## Growth Principle

The trust grows by cultivation, not extraction.

Growth is not measured only by attention, revenue, followers, users, token price, repository count, automation, speed, or public visibility. Growth is measured by whether the trust becomes more truthful, useful, resilient, accountable, humane, local-first, interoperable, and life-giving over time.

Every contribution should help the tree become healthier. Every branch should remain connected to the trunk. Every fruit should be traceable to real work. Every new seed should carry the constitutional pattern forward without erasing local freedom.

## The Living Pattern

The Tree of Life has seven primary layers:

1. Seed — origin, identity, consent, purpose, and first covenant.
2. Roots — values, relationships, history, skills, place, records, and nourishment.
3. Trunk — enduring mission, governance, continuity, and stewardship path.
4. Branches — projects, repositories, businesses, ministries, research, communities, and collaborations.
5. Leaves — daily actions, tasks, documents, commits, services, meetings, and learning.
6. Fruit — completed value that nourishes others.
7. New Seeds — future trusts, projects, repositories, communities, and opportunities born from completed fruit.

Each layer should be documented enough that a future steward can understand what exists, why it exists, what is active, what is complete, what is private, what is public, and what should happen next.

## Roots

Roots are the foundations that nourish the trust below the surface.

Roots may include:

- values and ethical commitments;
- family and trusted relationships;
- local place, Green Zone, and community context;
- faith, philosophy, doctrine, culture, and memory;
- skills, trades, education, lived experience, and professional knowledge;
- legal, financial, operational, and historical records;
- private archives and institutional memory;
- contributors, mentors, advisors, and support networks;
- lessons learned through failure, repair, recovery, and service.

Roots do not need to be public to be real. Many of the most important roots should remain private, protected, and locally stewarded.

The AI steward may help organize roots, but it must not expose private roots without consent.

## Trunk

The trunk is the enduring mission and continuity of the trust.

It connects the seed to every branch and keeps the trust from scattering into unrelated activity.

The trunk includes:

- mission;
- governance;
- decision rhythm;
- continuity planning;
- stewardship commitments;
- operating principles;
- repository standards;
- privacy and consent boundaries;
- records and auditability;
- review cycles;
- amendment process.

For this trust, the trunk begins with the declared purpose:

`{{TRUST_PURPOSE}}`

A healthy trunk is strong enough to support branches and flexible enough to grow.

If a project, repository, business, ministry, or collaboration cannot explain how it connects to the trunk, it should be reviewed before receiving more attention, resources, or public visibility.

## Branches

Branches are active expressions of the trust's mission.

Branches may include:

- software repositories;
- businesses;
- ministries;
- community projects;
- family stewardship efforts;
- educational work;
- research programs;
- publications;
- products and services;
- mutual-aid efforts;
- local missions;
- Federation collaborations;
- Living Atlas nodes;
- economic experiments;
- AI agents or workflows;
- infrastructure systems.

Every branch should have a clear purpose, steward, maturity level, visibility level, and relationship to the trunk.

A branch may be private, shared, public, experimental, archived, active, mature, or deprecated.

Branches should not be allowed to drain the tree without producing learning, service, repair, or fruit.

## Leaves

Leaves are daily work.

Leaves may include:

- tasks;
- notes;
- commits;
- issue updates;
- pull requests;
- documents;
- conversations;
- invoices;
- meetings;
- service calls;
- research notes;
- training sessions;
- checklists;
- local actions;
- design drafts;
- test results;
- small acts of care, repair, and contribution.

Leaves are often temporary, but they are not meaningless. Daily work is how the trust breathes.

The trust should not despise small work. Small faithful actions become the canopy through which the tree receives light.

Leaves should be organized enough that active work can be reviewed, completed, archived, or converted into fruit.

## Fruit

Fruit is completed value that can nourish others.

Fruit may include:

- released software;
- finished documents;
- useful research;
- completed repairs;
- delivered services;
- paid work completed honestly;
- public goods;
- educational resources;
- resolved conflicts;
- lessons learned;
- community support;
- improved infrastructure;
- trustworthy records;
- revenue generated through lawful service;
- mentorship;
- new capabilities;
- healing, restoration, and increased resilience.

Fruit should be identifiable. A trust should be able to say what was completed, who it helped, what evidence supports it, what value was created, what remains imperfect, and what can be learned from it.

Fruit is not only money. Money may be one kind of fruit, but it is not the root, trunk, or final purpose of the tree.

## New Seeds

New seeds are future growth born from fruit.

New seeds may become:

- new trusts;
- new repositories;
- new branches;
- new projects;
- new communities;
- new educational programs;
- new businesses;
- new ministries;
- new public-good systems;
- new tools or protocols;
- new local Green Zone initiatives;
- new Federation nodes.

A new seed should carry enough of the constitutional pattern to grow responsibly while adapting to its own local context.

Replication should not mean cloning control. The Federation grows best when each new seed inherits the genome while remaining locally alive.

## Maturity Levels

The trust may classify each branch, mission, repository, or fruit by maturity.

### Seeded

The idea has been named and recorded but is not yet active.

### Germinating

The idea has early notes, experiments, or conversations but remains unstable.

### Rooting

The work is gathering context, records, relationships, and basic structure.

### Growing

The work is active and producing leaves through daily tasks and visible progress.

### Flowering

The work is approaching public usefulness, review, launch, publication, or service delivery.

### Fruiting

The work has produced completed value that can nourish others.

### Seeding

The completed fruit is producing new opportunities, trusts, repositories, projects, or communities.

### Archived

The work is no longer active but remains preserved for memory, learning, provenance, or future revival.

### Pruned

The work has been intentionally stopped, reduced, or removed because it no longer serves the mission, consumes too much energy, creates risk, or conflicts with the trust's values.

## Pruning

Pruning is faithful stewardship.

The trust should periodically review branches, leaves, commitments, tools, public claims, AI workflows, economic experiments, and Federation connections.

A branch may need pruning when it:

- no longer serves the trunk;
- exposes private information unnecessarily;
- creates legal, financial, safety, or reputation risk;
- consumes resources without learning or fruit;
- distracts from higher responsibilities;
- depends on unclear authority;
- has become performative rather than useful;
- has fulfilled its purpose and should be archived.

Pruning should be documented where appropriate. Ending a branch honestly is better than pretending it is alive.

## Seasonal Rhythm

The trust should recognize seasons.

Not every season is for expansion. Some seasons are for rooting, repair, review, rest, pruning, learning, consolidation, or protection.

A healthy governance rhythm may include:

- daily or weekly leaf review;
- monthly branch review;
- quarterly fruit review;
- annual root and trunk review;
- emergency review when safety, privacy, or mission integrity is at risk.

The AI steward may assist with seasonal review by summarizing records, identifying neglected branches, listing completed fruit, and proposing next actions.

## Tree Records

The trust should preserve Tree of Life records in a way future stewards can inspect.

Useful records include:

- branch lists;
- mission logs;
- repository maps;
- decision logs;
- contribution ledgers;
- release notes;
- service records;
- fruit summaries;
- archived branches;
- lessons learned;
- new seed proposals.

Records should distinguish active, completed, archived, private, shared, and public work.

## Relationship to the Living Atlas

The Tree of Life connects to the Living Atlas through place, mission, and visibility.

The Green Zone is where the trust experiences its local present. Branches may appear on the Living Atlas when the trust intentionally publishes them as public or shared nodes.

A branch may be visible at different levels: private only, trusted circle, region-level, city-level, public project location, public business location, repository-only, or Federation-wide.

The Tree should never require public exposure of precise private location.

## Relationship to Economy

The economy should serve the Tree of Life.

Economic activity should help nourish roots, strengthen the trunk, support branches, sustain daily leaves, reward useful contribution, distribute fruit, and plant new seeds.

The trust should avoid economic systems that detach value from real work, convert people into extraction targets, or treat speculative numbers as fruit before useful value exists.

## Relationship to AI Stewardship

The AI steward may help maintain the Tree of Life by:

- organizing active branches;
- summarizing leaves;
- identifying fruit;
- drafting new seed proposals;
- detecting neglected commitments;
- warning about overgrowth or unclear scope;
- preserving handoff summaries;
- mapping repositories to branches;
- helping review seasonal progress.

The AI steward must not decide the trust's mission, publish private branches, create economic claims, or prune human responsibilities without approval.

## Practical Review Questions

When reviewing the Tree of Life, the trust should ask:

1. What is our seed and has it remained clear?
2. Are our roots nourished and protected?
3. Is our trunk strong enough to support our branches?
4. Which branches are active, neglected, fruitful, risky, or ready for pruning?
5. What leaves are being produced through daily work?
6. What fruit has actually been completed?
7. Who has been nourished by the fruit?
8. What new seeds should be planted?
9. What should remain private?
10. What should be shared with the Federation?

## Amendment

This Tree of Life constitution may be amended through the trust's governance process.

Amendments should clarify growth, stewardship, records, review rhythms, privacy, Federation visibility, maturity labels, and the relationship between work and fruit.

Each amendment should be documented and versioned so future stewards can understand how the trust's growth model matured over time.

## Closing Declaration

This trust commits to grow as a living tree rather than a hollow structure. It will honor its seed, nourish its roots, strengthen its trunk, tend its branches, respect its leaves, recognize its fruit, and plant new seeds only in ways that preserve dignity, truthfulness, stewardship, privacy, consent, and long-term flourishing.
