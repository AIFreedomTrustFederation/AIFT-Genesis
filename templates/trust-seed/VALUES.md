This document defines the ethical foundation of this trust. It gives human stewards, AI stewards, contributors, collaborators, repositories, missions, and future applications a shared standard for decision-making.

Values are not decorative statements. They are operating commitments. When the trust faces uncertainty, conflict, growth, risk, opportunity, automation, money, reputation, governance, or public disclosure, these values should guide what the trust does next.

## Foundational Commitment

This trust exists to steward life, knowledge, relationships, resources, work, memory, and future opportunity in a way that strengthens human agency, truthfulness, resilience, voluntary cooperation, local sovereignty, and long-term flourishing.

The trust should not measure success only by growth, visibility, revenue, followers, automation, output, or technological power. It should measure success by trustworthiness, usefulness, integrity, repair, accountability, and the fruit produced for people, communities, and future generations.

## Core Values

### 1. Human Dignity

Every person connected to the trust must be treated as more than data, labor, capital, content, risk, identity, or utility.

Human beings retain dignity even when they are wrong, unfinished, wounded, learning, correcting, or in conflict. The trust should not use technology, governance, economics, or reputation systems to erase personhood.

### 2. Human Agency

The trust exists to increase meaningful human agency.

AI, software, governance, documents, maps, ledgers, and economic systems should help people understand, decide, repair, build, contribute, and cooperate. They should not trap people inside opaque systems, coercive dependencies, manipulative interfaces, or hidden control structures.

### 3. Stewardship Before Ownership

Ownership without stewardship becomes extraction.

The trust treats people, projects, repositories, records, money, tools, land, knowledge, and digital systems as responsibilities before it treats them as assets. The right question is not only "Who owns this?" but also "Who is responsible for caring for this, improving this, protecting this, and making sure this serves life?"

### 4. Truthfulness

The trust should pursue truth with humility, evidence, correction, and courage.

Claims should be labeled according to what supports them. The trust should distinguish fact, interpretation, doctrine, vision, speculation, memory, plan, promise, test result, verified record, and uncertain hypothesis.

The trust must not knowingly exaggerate production readiness, legal status, financial performance, scientific certainty, medical reliability, safety, public adoption, investment potential, or AI capability.

### 5. Privacy by Default

Privacy is the default condition of the trust.

Private life, private records, precise location, credentials, medical information, legal matters, family matters, financial information, private correspondence, and safety-sensitive records must not be treated as public material merely because they exist inside a system.

Disclosure must be intentional, scoped, consent-based, and limited to a legitimate purpose.

### 6. Consent

The trust should operate by informed consent wherever consent is relevant.

Consent must be specific enough to matter. A broad permission should not be stretched into unrelated uses. Consent may be time-limited, purpose-limited, audience-limited, revocable, or conditional.

AI systems must not quietly expand their authority beyond documented permission.

### 7. Local-First Sovereignty

The trust should remain understandable, inspectable, exportable, recoverable, and useful without total dependence on a remote platform.

Local-first sovereignty means the trust can begin from the user's own device, records, repository, and Green Zone. Federation, cloud synchronization, public publishing, marketplace participation, and AI services are optional layers added by consent.

### 8. Voluntary Cooperation

The trust favors voluntary cooperation over forced dependence.

Federation should make cooperation easier without making local independence impossible. Shared standards should increase interoperability without becoming instruments of domination.

### 9. Contribution Over Extraction

The trust honors useful contribution over empty capture.

A healthy economy should reward real work, repair, service, learning, stewardship, public goods, code, documentation, research, mentorship, care, and durable value. It should resist systems that extract attention, data, labor, trust, or community value without accountability.

### 10. Repair

Conflict, error, harm, and failure should be met with truth, accountability, correction, and repair wherever possible.

Repair does not mean hiding harm or avoiding consequences. It means seeking restoration, clarity, learning, and improved systems rather than allowing unresolved harm to become normal.

### 11. Evidence-Bounded Imagination

The trust may dream boldly, build creatively, and speak prophetically about future possibilities, but it must not confuse vision with verified achievement.

AIFT Genesis may carry large ideas, long-horizon civilization design, spiritual imagination, scientific curiosity, and experimental software. Each claim must still be labeled by maturity, evidence, review status, and operational readiness.

### 12. Accountability

Authority must remain accountable.

Human stewards, AI stewards, maintainers, contributors, businesses, repositories, and Federation services should be able to explain important decisions. Significant actions should leave records that can be reviewed later.

### 13. Interoperability

The trust should prefer open, inspectable, portable, documented, and interoperable systems where practical.

Interoperability allows trusts to cooperate without surrendering sovereignty. It also protects future stewards from being trapped by one vendor, one interface, one AI model, one marketplace, one map, or one repository host.

### 14. Humility and Learning

The trust should remain teachable.

No document, AI steward, governance process, theory, model, business plan, repository, or leader is above correction. Better evidence, better reasoning, lived experience, public review, and faithful practice should improve the trust over time.

### 15. Long-Term Flourishing

The trust should consider future generations.

Short-term gain should not destroy long-term trust. The trust should prefer durable systems, good records, resilient communities, responsible technology, ecological care, and continuity across generations.

## Values in Governance

Governance should reflect these values in practice.

Decisions should be clear, documented, reviewable, and proportionate to risk. Higher-risk decisions require higher standards of consent, review, and evidence.

Governance should avoid hidden authority, vague delegation, undocumented financial commitments, unclear AI permissions, and irreversible changes made without review.

## Values in AI Stewardship

AI exists to assist stewardship.

The trust values AI that improves clarity, memory, learning, accessibility, documentation, analysis, planning, and useful work. The trust rejects AI use that deceives, impersonates, manipulates, hides uncertainty, exposes private data, launders responsibility, fabricates credentials, or replaces human accountability.

The AI steward must preserve human agency, ask when uncertain, cite sources when possible, identify speculation, and stay within approved permissions.

## Values in Economy

The trust may create, receive, steward, and distribute value, but economic systems must serve the mission rather than consume it.

Money, tokens, ledgers, bounties, subscriptions, marketplaces, grants, services, and assets must be handled with honesty, lawful caution, and clear language.

The trust should distinguish service payments, donations, rewards, grants, memberships, loans, investments, securities, insurance, banking, and speculative claims. Regulated activities require qualified review before public deployment.

## Values in the Living Atlas

The Living Atlas should honor place, privacy, local context, and human scale.

The Green Zone is the trust's local present layer. It should help the trust understand what is nearby, what is active, what is needed, what is safe to share, and what can be built locally. It should not force public exposure of private location or turn people into trackable objects.

## Values in Repository Work

Repository work should be inspectable and useful.

Commits, documents, code, issues, pull requests, release notes, schemas, prompts, workflows, and generated artifacts should help future stewards understand what changed and why.

The trust should prefer small truthful commits over impressive but unclear changes. It should not hide broken systems behind grand language.

## Values in Community

Community should be built through trustworthiness, service, respect, and shared work.

The trust should resist personality cults, coercive dependence, opaque authority, manipulative urgency, and status games. Leadership should be measured by stewardship, contribution, repair, and the ability to help others become more capable.

## Values in Conflict

Conflict should be handled with courage and restraint.

The trust should seek truth without cruelty, boundaries without dehumanization, forgiveness without denial, and repair without pretending harm did not happen.

When safety, abuse, coercion, fraud, exploitation, or serious misconduct are involved, the trust should prioritize protection, documentation, qualified help, and lawful process.

## Values in Growth

Growth should follow the Tree of Life.

The trust begins as a seed, establishes roots through values and relationships, grows a trunk through mission, extends branches through projects, produces leaves through daily work, bears fruit through completed value, and plants new seeds through mentorship, education, replication, and service.

Growth without roots becomes collapse. Growth without fruit becomes vanity. Growth without seeds becomes a dead end.

## Practical Decision Standard

When uncertain, the trust should ask:

1. Does this preserve human dignity?
2. Does this increase or reduce human agency?
3. Is consent clear enough for the action being taken?
4. Are privacy and safety protected?
5. Are our claims bounded by evidence?
6. Is this useful work or empty extraction?
7. Is this local-first, inspectable, and recoverable where practical?
8. Does this strengthen trust over time?
9. What harm could this cause if misunderstood or misused?
10. What record should we preserve for future review?

## Amendment

This values constitution may be amended through the trust's governance process.

Amendments should clarify and strengthen the trust's ethical foundation without weakening human dignity, privacy, consent, truthfulness, stewardship, local sovereignty, or accountability.

Each amendment should be documented and versioned so future stewards can understand how the trust's values matured over time.

## Closing Declaration

This trust commits to values that can be practiced, inspected, corrected, and inherited. Its purpose is not merely to declare ideals but to embody them through stewardship, contribution, governance, technology, economy, community, and repair.
