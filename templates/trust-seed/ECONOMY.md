This document defines how this trust understands contribution, value, exchange, compensation, public goods, stewardship, missions, bounties, shared resources, marketplace activity, and economic records.

The economy of the trust must serve the Tree of Life. Money, tokens, ledgers, rewards, grants, memberships, assets, marketplaces, and financial tools are instruments of stewardship. They are not the mission itself.

## Economic Purpose

The purpose of the trust economy is to help useful work become visible, supported, completed, reviewed, rewarded, shared, and converted into fruit.

A healthy trust economy should:

- honor real contribution;
- support lawful service and exchange;
- sustain people doing useful work;
- preserve truthful records;
- fund public goods where possible;
- strengthen local resilience;
- avoid extraction without accountability;
- distinguish verified value from speculation;
- protect privacy and consent;
- remain honest about legal, financial, tax, and regulatory uncertainty.

The trust should not create an economy that rewards hype more than service, speculation more than usefulness, or control more than stewardship.

## Foundational Economic Principles

This trust inherits the following economic principles:

1. Value begins with real contribution, service, care, repair, learning, stewardship, and useful work.
2. Economic systems must preserve human dignity and agency.
3. The trust must not overstate financial performance, investment value, adoption, liquidity, legal status, or production readiness.
4. Contribution should be documented when it affects compensation, reputation, governance, or public claims.
5. Economic participation should be voluntary, transparent, and based on informed consent.
6. Private financial data remains private unless intentionally shared for a defined purpose.
7. Regulated activity requires qualified review before public deployment or promotion.
8. The economy should nourish roots, strengthen the trunk, support branches, sustain leaves, recognize fruit, and plant new seeds.
9. Public goods and local resilience are legitimate forms of value.
10. No economic tool should become more important than the people and communities it exists to serve.

## Contribution

A contribution is useful work or support that helps the trust fulfill its mission.

Contributions may include:

- code;
- documentation;
- research;
- design;
- project management;
- testing;
- issue triage;
- repository maintenance;
- teaching;
- mentoring;
- spiritual care or community care where appropriate;
- local repairs;
- delivery work;
- food, logistics, or disaster support;
- business development;
- customer service;
- infrastructure work;
- governance participation;
- funding;
- equipment;
- facilities;
- public goods;
- conflict repair;
- recordkeeping;
- AI-assisted workflow development.

Contribution does not automatically create payment, ownership, employment, partnership, governance authority, token entitlement, intellectual property rights, or future benefit unless those terms are documented.

## Contribution Ledger

A contribution ledger is a record of useful work.

A healthy contribution record should include:

- contributor or responsible party;
- date or period of contribution;
- branch, mission, repository, or project served;
- description of work;
- evidence or link to the work;
- review status;
- completion status;
- compensation, reward, or recognition if applicable;
- privacy classification;
- notes about uncertainty, dispute, or follow-up.

The ledger should support accountability and recognition without becoming a dehumanizing score, social credit system, or coercive surveillance mechanism.

The trust may record value flows, including:

- funds received;

## Missions

A mission is a defined task or effort that creates value for the trust, a branch, a community, a repository, a customer, a public-good effort, or the Federation.

Each mission should define:

- mission title;
- purpose;
- branch or Tree of Life relationship;
- steward or responsible party;
- visibility level;
- location precision if relevant;
- required skills or resources;
- privacy and safety considerations;
- acceptance criteria;
- evidence required for completion;
- reward, payment, recognition, or no-reward status;
- verifier or review process;
- completion record;
- fruit produced.

A mission should not promise compensation or public recognition unless those terms are actually authorized.

## Bounties and Rewards

A bounty is an offer of reward for completing defined work.

A bounty should be specific enough to prevent confusion. It should identify:

- task scope;
- deliverable;
- acceptance criteria;
- amount or form of reward;
- payment method if applicable;
- who may approve completion;
- deadline if any;
- tax or reporting responsibility where relevant;
- dispute process;
- cancellation conditions.

The trust should not publish deceptive, vague, unfunded, or misleading bounties.

Rewards may be monetary, reputational, educational, relational, access-based, service-based, or public-good recognition. Non-monetary rewards should not be described as financial value unless they are actually redeemable under documented terms.

## Services, Offerings, and Marketplace Activity

The trust may offer lawful services, products, subscriptions, memberships, educational materials, software, consulting, local work, food-related services, repair work, logistics, research, community support, or other mission-aligned offerings.

Marketplace listings should be truthful and should include relevant terms such as:

- what is offered;
- who provides it;
- price or donation structure;
- delivery method;
- location or service area if public;
- refund or cancellation terms where relevant;
- limitations;
- maturity level;
- safety or compliance considerations;
- contact method.

The trust must not imply licensing, inspection, insurance, certification, legal authority, medical safety, investment status, or regulatory approval unless those claims are true and documented.

## Donations, Grants, and Public Goods

The trust may receive or distribute donations, grants, sponsorships, mutual-aid support, or public-good funding when lawful and transparent.

Donation and grant language should distinguish between:

- gifts with no exchange promised;
- restricted gifts for a defined purpose;
- grants with reporting obligations;
- sponsorships with public recognition;
- memberships with defined benefits;
- purchases of goods or services;
- investments or securities requiring qualified review.

Public goods should be documented so contributors and future stewards can understand what was produced, who it served, how it was funded, and what remains needed.

## Tokens, Credits, Points, and Digital Assets

The trust may experiment with tokens, credits, contribution points, badges, digital assets, ledgers, or reputation markers only when their meaning is clearly defined.

A token, credit, badge, or point system must state whether it is:

- symbolic recognition;
- internal accounting;
- access permission;
- contribution record;
- redeemable credit;
- loyalty reward;
- governance signal;
- digital collectible;
- payment instrument;
- investment-like asset;
- regulated financial product requiring review.

The trust must not market speculative value, returns, profit expectations, tradability, liquidity, appreciation, or investment opportunity without qualified legal and compliance review.

## Cooperative Value

The trust may participate in cooperative value creation.

Cooperative economics may include shared tools, shared customers, shared missions, shared infrastructure, joint purchasing, mutual aid, worker participation, community-supported services, revenue-sharing agreements, or cooperative ownership structures.

Any cooperative arrangement should define governance, responsibilities, rights, payment terms, dispute process, records, and exit conditions.

Cooperation should not be used to hide unclear obligations or avoid accountability.

## Local Economy and Green Zone

The trust's economy begins in the Green Zone: the local present layer where needs, skills, resources, relationships, and missions become visible.

Local economic activity may include:

- repairs;
- deliveries;
- food distribution;
- local commerce;
- mobile services;
- community support;
- garden work;
- equipment sharing;
- education;
- local events;
- mutual-aid missions;
- business development;
- public-good projects.

Location visibility must respect the Living Atlas privacy model. A local opportunity should not expose private homes, vulnerable people, sensitive routes, or safety-critical context without consent.

## AI Steward Role in Economy

The AI steward may assist the economy by:

- drafting invoices, estimates, offers, mission descriptions, and bounty terms;
- organizing contribution ledgers;
- summarizing value flows;
- reviewing language for overclaims;
- identifying missing acceptance criteria;
- preparing marketplace listings;
- tracking public-good outputs;
- mapping economic activity to Tree of Life fruit;
- flagging legal, tax, financial, licensing, or regulatory uncertainty.

The AI steward must not:

- fabricate financial records;
- claim payments were made or received without evidence;
- create legal or tax determinations;
- market investments or regulated financial products as safe or approved;
- promise returns;
- change payment terms without approval;
- publish private financial information;
- initiate payments or commitments without authorization.

## Financial Records

The trust should preserve material financial records according to lawful and practical needs.

Useful records may include:

- receipts;
- invoices;
- estimates;
- contracts;
- grants;
- donation records;
- payment confirmations;
- contribution records;
- reimbursement logs;
- asset inventories;
- tax-relevant summaries;
- public-good funding records;
- dispute records.

Private financial records should remain private unless disclosure is required or intentionally authorized.

## Risk, Compliance, and Review

Economic systems can create legal, tax, financial, safety, privacy, and reputation risk.

The trust should seek qualified professional review when activity involves:

- securities or investment-like offerings;
- lending;
- insurance;
- banking or money transmission;
- payroll or employment classification;
- contractor classification;
- tax filings;
- charitable fundraising rules;
- food, health, safety, or licensing requirements;
- custody of funds for others;
- regulated professional services;
- public financial claims;
- tokens or tradable digital assets;
- large commitments or long-term obligations.

The trust may use AI for planning and education, but qualified human review is required where professional judgment or regulated compliance is needed.

## Economic Maturity Labels

Economic activity should be labeled by maturity.

Suggested labels include:

- concept;
- draft;
- internal pilot;
- local pilot;
- active service;
- public listing;
- revenue producing;
- grant funded;
- public good;
- cooperative;
- under review;
- paused;
- archived;
- disputed;
- regulated review required.

Maturity labels help the trust avoid presenting experiments as stable systems.

## Disputes and Repair

Economic disputes should be handled through records, clarity, accountability, and repair.

The trust should review:

- what was promised;
- what was delivered;
- what evidence exists;
- what payment or reward was expected;
- what terms were unclear;
- what harm occurred;
- what repair is possible;
- what should be changed to prevent recurrence.

When disputes involve serious legal, financial, safety, employment, consumer, or regulatory issues, the trust should seek qualified help.

## Relationship to the Tree of Life

The economy must serve the Tree of Life.

Roots need nourishment. The trunk needs continuity. Branches need support. Leaves need coordination. Fruit needs recognition and distribution. New seeds need responsible planting.

Economic systems that produce money while damaging trust, privacy, dignity, consent, or truthfulness are unhealthy even if they appear profitable.

## Practical Review Questions

Before creating or publishing economic activity, the trust should ask:

1. What real value is being created?
2. Who is contributing?
3. Who benefits?
4. What is promised?
5. What is not promised?
6. Is payment, reward, or recognition clearly defined?
7. Are our claims evidence-bounded?
8. Is any regulated activity involved?
9. What records must be preserved?
10. Does this nourish the Tree of Life?

## Amendment

This economy constitution may be amended through the trust's governance process.

Amendments should improve clarity, lawfulness, transparency, contribution recognition, privacy, consent, value flow, dispute handling, and the relationship between economy and stewardship.

Each amendment should be documented and versioned.

## Closing Declaration

This trust commits to an economy of stewardship rather than extraction. It will use money, ledgers, missions, bounties, assets, services, marketplaces, and cooperative systems as tools to support useful work, local resilience, public goods, lawful exchange, and completed fruit that can nourish people, communities, and future generations.
